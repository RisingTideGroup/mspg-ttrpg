Cinder burnt the left side of their body on a pressure valve at age nine — deep in the Forge Quarter, where children weren't supposed to be, but nobody has ever talked sense into Cinder Goss. Three generations of Blastwicks carried the postal route between the Forge Quarter and the Docks at regulated, careful, unremarkable speed. Pip grew up timing the route and finding the cuts. By twelve they had shaved eleven minutes off the official path. By fifteen they'd been formally asked to stop. They took the severance, bought a second-hand fuel cell, and entered their first unsanctioned race that same afternoon — still in the postal jacket. The paperwork still says postal vehicle. They haven't updated it. They find this funny.

Mirabel spent four years as a cartographer's apprentice drawing streets she wasn't allowed to run. Every map had annotations in the margins — optimal routes, blind corners, sections of road that looked straight but weren't. Her master called them distracting. She called them the actual point. She left when she had mapped every racing circuit in Cogsworth to within three inches. She updates the notebook after every race, including ones she wasn't in. She has never lost a hairpin she had annotated in advance. The Undercroft Tunnel is the only section with no notes. She refuses. She says you have to feel it — and when she does, when the line appears in the dark and the construct tracks through the S-curve clean, she calls it quietly: The Line.

Her father collected district taxes. Ophelia collected information about who paid late, who paid in goods, and whose books didn't match. By sixteen she was running the unofficial market beneath the official one — nothing illegal, technically, just extremely well-informed. There was a family meeting. She took her cut, bought a racing licence, and has been operating in plain sight ever since — which she finds less dangerous and considerably more interesting than hiding. She's charged three competing crews for information about the Premium Package this season alone. The best moves are always In Plain Sight. Everyone sees them. Nobody believes them until it's done.

Character
Fen ran the numbers for half the racing crews in Cogsworth before they decided the better bet was entering themselves. A working bookie by trade — odds, spreads, side action on everything from lap times to which vendor runs out of stock first. They know the Bazaar the way most people know their own pockets. Every race they've attended as a bookmaker has ended up as a line in the notebook, annotated with what the favourite did wrong. At some point they started annotating what they would have done instead. Eventually that felt like a reason to find out. Fen still takes the occasional side bet during Act I. They consider this research. Before every big move they flip to a page, run a finger down the numbers, and say it straight: Already in the Book.
