Three generations of Blastwicks carried the postal route between the Forge Quarter and the Docks at regulated, careful, unremarkable speed. Pip grew up timing the route and finding the cuts. By twelve they had shaved eleven minutes off the official path. By fifteen they'd been formally asked to stop. They took the severance, bought a second-hand fuel cell, and entered their first unsanctioned race that same afternoon — still in the postal jacket. The paperwork still says postal vehicle. They haven't updated it. They find this funny.

Mirabel spent four years as a cartographer's apprentice drawing streets she wasn't allowed to run. Every map had annotations in the margins — optimal routes, blind corners, sections of road that looked straight but weren't. Her master called them distracting. She called them the actual point. She left when she had mapped every racing circuit in Cogsworth to within three inches. She updates the notebook after every race, including ones she wasn't in. She has never lost a hairpin she had annotated in advance. The Undercroft Tunnel is the only section with no notes. She refuses. She says you have to feel it — and when she does, when the line appears in the dark and the construct tracks through the S-curve clean, she calls it quietly: The Line.
